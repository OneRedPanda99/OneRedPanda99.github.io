Fusion 360, FDM printing, and a lot of failed first attempts. Learning how things are actually made.

How it started

A kid at school had one.
I wanted one.

I saw a kid using a 3D printer at school and that was it. I wanted to make my own things — not download models, not buy parts, actually design and produce them myself. So I figured out how to make that happen.

It started with Fusion 360. Learning parametric CAD from scratch, understanding how dimensions and constraints relate to each other, and slowly building an instinct for what's actually manufacturable versus what just looks fine on screen.

What I've made

Functional first,
everything else second.

Some of it is practice. Some of it I actually use. The stuff I care about most required real iteration — printing, testing, adjusting tolerances, printing again.

Mechanical
Ball Bearings
Printed entirely to learn tolerance. Too loose and the outer race wobbles. Too tight and it won't spin. Getting the clearance right took multiple iterations — but once it clicked, I understood how to think about fit for every part after that.
Everyday Use
Clips, Mounts, Holders
Cable clips, cup holders, shelf mounts. The unglamorous stuff that actually gets used. These are the prints that test whether a design survives contact with the real world — stress, repetition, not breaking the third time someone uses it.
Wearables
Masks
Designing around a human face is a different problem than a flat bracket. You're dealing with compound curves, asymmetry, and a part that has to fit a person. Pushed me to get better at surface modeling in Fusion 360.
Practice
Batarangs and Sharp Geometry
Not every print needs a purpose. Sharp internal angles, thin features, symmetry — these are the things that break prints and expose what you don't know about your slicer settings. Good practice before designing something that actually has to work.

The Learning Curve

The print fell off the bed mid-way.
Turns out you have to clean it first.

My first real failure was a print that detached mid-job. The bed wasn't clean, so the filament wouldn't adhere. The fix was obvious once I knew it — IPA, a scraper, a few minutes of prep. But you only learn that by ruining a print first. That's the whole deal with 3D printing: every failure is the cheapest way to learn something that would've taken hours to read about and still not fully understand.

Where this goes

Printing parts is the
beginning, not the point.

The reason I care about 3D printing isn't the printing itself — it's that designing and making physical things from scratch is the core skill underneath mechatronics and engineering. A printed part that just sits on a shelf is useless. A printed part that holds a sensor mount on a robot that actually moves is interesting.

That's the direction. The FTC robotics program I started at Cardinal Newman is where these skills actually get used — designing parts in Fusion 360 that have to survive competition, mounting hardware, holding tolerances under load.

Applied in Robotics
Custom mounts, brackets, and structural parts for the FTC robotics program — designed in Fusion 360, printed and tested under real competition conditions.
Goal: Mechatronics
Mechanical design, electronics, and software working together. 3D printing is the mechanical side — understanding how to make parts that fit and function is the foundation everything else builds on.
